Diversify Your Repair Shop: Tablets, Laptops and Consoles

Your shop already has the premises, the tools and the customers. Repairing phones only leaves money on the table: tablets, laptops and consoles use the same hands, the same workbench and many of the same suppliers.

Phone repair has become fiercely competitive. In many towns there is a shop every few streets, screen margins keep shrinking and customers compare prices in two clicks. Meanwhile, that same customer has a tablet with a cracked screen at home, a laptop that won't turn on and a console that shuts itself off from overheating — and no idea where to take them.

Diversifying does not mean reinventing your business. It means using what you already have — premises, tools, reputation and a customer base that trusts you — to capture repairs that are slipping away today. This guide covers where to start, what you need and how not to lose money in the process.

1. Why diversify your repair shop

The underlying case is simple: your fixed costs are already paid. Rent, electricity, insurance and your presence in the shop do not change because you also take in a tablet. Every new repair you accept sits on top of a structure you already support, so the extra income drops almost straight to your margin.

  • More income with the same resources: same shop, same base tools and, in many cases, the same parts and consumables suppliers.
  • Less reliance on a single line: if phone repair demand dips in summer or screen prices rise, other lines cushion the blow.
  • Higher average ticket: replacing a laptop battery or repairing a console's HDMI port usually bills more than an ordinary phone repair.
  • Stronger loyalty: a customer who brings you their phone, tablet and console is far less likely to switch to a competitor.

Key idea: you don't have to open everything at once. Start with one device type, master its workflow and add the next one once the first is already profitable.

2. Which devices to add first

Not every device performs the same or demands the same learning curve. This is a sensible order for most shops that already repair phones:

DeviceMost common faultsLearning curveWhy start here
TabletsScreen, battery, charging portLowAlmost identical to a phone, just bigger
SmartwatchesScreen, battery, glassMediumSmall parts, good margin
LaptopsBattery, keyboard, screen, cleaning, SSDMediumHigh ticket and heavy demand
ConsolesHDMI port, controller drift, cooling, thermal pasteMedium-highLittle local competition, loyal customers

Tablets: the natural next step

A tablet is essentially a large phone. The opening procedures, screen replacement and battery swaps are very similar to what you already do. It is the lowest-risk addition and the one that starts billing soonest.

Laptops: the biggest runway

The volume here is enormous: battery swaps, keyboards, screens, internal cleaning, HDD-to-SSD upgrades and memory expansions. Many of these jobs require no microsoldering and carry a higher average ticket than a phone.

Consoles: less competition

Repairing a console's HDMI port, a controller with stick drift or performing a full thermal service are much-requested jobs with little local supply. Some faults do require microsoldering, so start with the mechanical work (controllers, cooling, thermal paste) and level up when you feel comfortable.

3. What you need to get started

The good news is you already have the essentials. These are the three areas to cover:

Parts and suppliers

  • Find suppliers specialising in tablet screens, laptop batteries and console parts
  • Start on a per-order basis so you don't tie up cash in stock that isn't turning yet
  • Offer part quality tiers (compatible, refurbished, original) on these devices too
  • Keep part references and compatibilities by model so you order without mistakes

Training

  • Teardown guides by model (there are huge communities and channels for laptops and consoles)
  • Practise first on test or spare units before touching a customer's device
  • Make a formal jump to microsoldering only when the volume justifies it

Tools

  • Extra precision screwdrivers (laptops use more screw types)
  • Hot-air station and a good supply of thermal paste for consoles and laptops
  • Larger suction cups and picks for tablet screens
  • Adjustable power supply and multimeter for diagnostics

Tip: don't buy every tool at once. Add each piece of equipment once the first repair that needs it has already been paid for; that way demand itself funds your investment.

4. How to set prices and warranties

The principle is the same as with phones: price = part cost + labour + margin, plus a coherent warranty. But each device has its own nuances.

  • Price the real time: opening a laptop or stripping a console takes longer than a phone. Charge labour based on the actual hours, not on phone-repair habits.
  • Always quote in writing: on expensive devices, a clear signed quote prevents arguments at pickup.
  • Diagnosis as a service: on laptops and consoles, diagnosis is real work. Charge a diagnosis fee that you deduct if the customer accepts the repair.
  • Tiered warranty: you can offer 3 months on compatible parts and extend it on higher-quality parts. Always make clear what it covers and what it doesn't (for example, later liquid damage).

Legal note: remember that the commercial warranty you offer sits alongside the statutory warranty set by your country's regulations. Check the mandatory minimum period before setting your terms.

5. Common mistakes when starting out

  • Accepting any repair from day one: saying yes to a liquid-damaged laptop board without experience is the fast track to an angry customer. Start with what you master.
  • Buying stock before demand: you tie up cash in parts that may not turn. Work per order until you know what recurs.
  • Copying the phone price: applying a phone-repair rate to a job that takes three times as long means working for free.
  • Not documenting the intake condition: a laptop or console has far more details (dents, missing screws, disk sectors). Photograph and note the condition on arrival.
  • Promising deadlines that depend on a part you don't have: with new suppliers, pad the deadline until you know their real delivery times.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need microsoldering to diversify?
Not to get started. Most tablet, laptop and console repairs are mechanical: screens, batteries, keyboards, charging ports, cooling and thermal paste. Microsoldering is a later step that only pays off once the volume justifies it.
Which device should I start with?
Tablets, because the procedure is almost identical to a phone and the risk is minimal. Then laptops for their high ticket and demand, and finally consoles, which face little competition but require a bit more skill.
Do I have to buy a lot of stock?
No. The recommendation is to work per order at first so you don't tie up cash. Once you identify which faults and models recur, keep a small stock of your fastest-moving parts.
How do I price these new repairs?
With the same formula as phones: part cost plus labour based on the actual hours, plus your margin. Be careful not to copy the phone rate: a laptop or console usually takes longer, and that time gets billed.
